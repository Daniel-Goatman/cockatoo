Walking the old harbour path at the end of the season

When the crowds go home, the shoreline gives the town back to the people who live there.

By a staff writer · 12 July 2026

By October the kiosks are shuttered and the rental bikes are gone, and the harbour path belongs to dog-walkers and the occasional runner. The Wasser turns a heavier shade of grey, and the light comes in low and sideways, the way it only does in the off-season.

There is a particular pleasure in taking the long Weg home when nothing is waiting for you. The fishermen mend nets they will not use until spring. A café that stays open year-round puts its chairs back out the moment the rain stops, as an act of faith.

Locals will tell you the town has two clocks. In summer, everything runs on the ferry timetable; in winter, on the tide. Either way, the Zeit here is measured in weather, not hours — nobody hurries, because there is nowhere to be that the sea cannot reach first.

Stay until the streetlights come on and you will see the whole shape of the place: the breakwater, the chapel, the terraces stacked against the hill. It is not a spectacular view. It is better than that — it is a familiar one.
